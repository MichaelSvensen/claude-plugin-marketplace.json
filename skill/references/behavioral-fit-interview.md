# Behavioral & Fit Interview — The Other Half of the Room

Case skill wins the case; **fit skill wins the offer.** Consulting and general-management interviews weight the "tell me about a time…" conversation as heavily as the case, because it's where the interviewer judges leadership, drive, teaming, and whether they'd staff you on a stressed client. This file covers how to build and deliver fit answers at the same bar as your structuring.

Use it when: preparing for or coaching an interview; drafting a personal-experience narrative; answering "why consulting / why this firm"; or converting a messy career story into a crisp, competency-mapped answer.

## The three anchor questions

Almost every fit conversation opens on some combination of these. Prepare all three cold.

1. **"Tell me about yourself" (the pitch).** 60–90 seconds. A through-line, not a résumé recital: where you started → 2–3 defining pivots/achievements (quantified) → why that leads here. End on a "hook" — a value proposition that maps to what they want. Avoid chronology-dumping.
2. **"Why consulting / why this firm."** Two distinct halves. *Why consulting:* the skills and exposure you want (structured problem-solving, pace, breadth) tied to concrete past experiences. *Why this firm:* culture, practice strengths, and people you've actually spoken to — specific, not generic. A strong answer is tailored to your prior experience and future aspiration, not a brochure paraphrase.
3. **"Why you / why should we take you."** Your differentiated 2–3 strengths, each with a proof story. This is where the story bank pays off.

## The story bank (build this first)

Don't prepare answers question-by-question — you'll be caught flat by novel phrasings. Instead build a **bank of 6–10 real stories**, each richly detailed, then map each to the competencies it can demonstrate. In the interview you *select and angle* a story to the question asked.

```
STORY BANK  →  maps to competencies (one story serves many questions)

  Story                    Lead | Achieve | Conflict | Failure | Influence | Ambiguity
  ----------------------   ----   -------   --------   -------   ---------   ---------
  1. Turned around club X    ●       ●          ·          ·         ●           ·
  2. Shipped project Y        ·       ●          ·          ●         ·           ●
  3. Disagreed with boss      ●       ·          ●          ·         ●           ·
  4. Missed a deadline        ·       ·          ·          ●         ·           ●
  5. Led team through crisis  ●       ●          ●          ·         ●           ●
  6. Convinced a skeptic      ·       ·          ●          ·         ●           ·
  ● = strong fit for that competency prompt
```

**Coverage test:** every column (leadership, achievement, conflict, failure, influence/persuasion, ambiguity/resilience, teamwork, ethics) should have at least two ● stories. Gaps are where you'll get exposed.

## Five delivery frameworks (pick by intent)

All five keep you structured under pressure. They differ in what they force you to foreground.

| Framework | Expands to | Best for | Forces you to… |
|---|---|---|---|
| **STAR** | Situation · Task · Action · Result | The default for any "tell me about a time" | Quantify the result; don't stop at the action |
| **START** | STAR + **T**akeaway | Growth/learning prompts | Name the explicit lesson and how you apply it now |
| **CARL** | Context · Action · Result · Learning | Reflective "what did you learn" prompts | Lead reflection, not narration |
| **PARADE** | Problem · Anticipated consequence · Role · Action · Decision rationale · End result | High-stakes leadership & judgment stories | Show the *reasoning* behind the decision, not just the deed |
| **COIN** | Context · Observation · Impact · Next step | Feedback / conflict / "difficult conversation" stories | Separate observed behavior from its impact before acting |

```
STAR (the spine)        PARADE (adds judgment)
  S  set the scene        P   the problem
  T  your specific task   A   what could go wrong
  A  what YOU did         R   your role (not "we")
  R  quantified result    A   the action you took
                          D   why you chose it  ← the differentiator
                          E   the end result
```

**Rule of thumb:** default to STAR; upgrade to PARADE when the story turns on a *judgment call* (the interviewer wants your decision-making, not your activity); use COIN for anything involving giving feedback or navigating conflict.

## Delivery standards (same bar as the case)

- **"I," not "we."** The interviewer is hiring you, not your team. State your specific contribution explicitly even in a team story.
- **Quantify the result.** "Grew participation from 40 to 180" beats "significantly increased participation." No number = no result.
- **Signpost.** "There were three things I did — first… second… third…" The same MECE, pyramid-led delivery you use in a case applies here.
- **Own the failure.** For failure/weakness prompts, pick a real failure, take genuine accountability (no humble-brags), and land the takeaway you now operate by. START/CARL are built for this.
- **60–90 seconds per story.** Long enough to show depth, short enough to invite follow-ups. Let the interviewer drill in.
- **Tailor, don't recite.** Angle the same bank story differently for "leadership" vs. "conflict." Reusing a story is fine; reusing the *emphasis* is the mistake.

## Common HRQ prompts to pre-stage

Map each to ≥1 bank story before you walk in:

- Leadership: *a time you led a team; led without authority; motivated a disengaged member.*
- Achievement: *your proudest accomplishment; a goal you set and hit.*
- Conflict/teaming: *a disagreement with a teammate or manager; a difficult stakeholder; a low-performing peer.*
- Failure/resilience: *a time you failed; a mistake and what you learned; handling a setback under pressure.*
- Influence: *convinced someone senior; changed a group's mind; drove a decision without control.*
- Ambiguity/drive: *ambiguous problem with no clear path; went beyond your remit; juggled competing priorities.*
- Ethics/values: *an ethical dilemma; pushed back on something you disagreed with.*

## Group discussions (GDs), where used

Some processes screen with a group discussion — a case GD (solve a problem as a group) or a "chairman/abstract" GD. What's assessed: whether you *structure* the discussion (offer a MECE frame early), *build* on others (not bulldoze), bring quiet members in, and *synthesize* toward the end. The winning move is rarely talking most — it's shaping the structure and closing with a crisp synthesis. Treat it as a live, multi-player version of the pyramid: frame → contribute evidence → converge.
