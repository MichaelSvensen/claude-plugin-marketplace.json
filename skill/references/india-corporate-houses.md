# India Corporate Houses — Business-House Intelligence

Deep, partner-level knowledge of India's major promoter-led business groups (the "houses"). Use this when a problem, deal, or interview touches an Indian conglomerate: an engagement inside a group company, a competitor/partner assessment, a general-management or leadership-programme interview, or any market-entry/M&A case where a house is the incumbent, acquirer, or gatekeeper.

**Why houses, not just companies.** In India, a disproportionate share of formal-sector profit, capital, and political access sits inside a dozen family-controlled groups. Reading a listed subsidiary in isolation misses the parent's cross-subsidy, capital-allocation logic, brand umbrella, and succession politics. You reason better when you hold the whole house.

### Naming & discretion

This file is **named-company intelligence** — profiling Tata, Reliance, Adani, and the rest by name is the entire point, exactly as the sector-vertical files name real firms. That is a **different lane** from the *Source Discretion* rule in `SKILL.md`, which governs the provenance of practice cases and guesstimates. Figures (market caps, revenues, headcount) drift — treat all financials here as **approximate, directional benchmarks**, label anything you extrapolate as `[ILLUSTRATIVE]`, and refresh hard numbers before quoting them to a client.

## House profiles

Each profile is deliberately compact: **snapshot → flagship entities → strategic thrust → what it tests / how to talk about it.** Financials are approximate and directional.

### Tata Group
- **Snapshot.** Founded 1868 (Jamsetji Tata); India's oldest and most respected house. Controlled by Tata Sons, itself ~66% owned by Tata philanthropic trusts — a governance structure with no single controlling family shareholder. Chairman (Tata Sons): N. Chandrasekaran. ~$150B+ group revenue, 100+ operating companies, ~30 listed.
- **Flagship entities.** TCS (the profit engine and one of the world's largest IT-services firms), Tata Motors (incl. Jaguar Land Rover and India EV leadership), Tata Steel, Tata Consumer, Titan, Tata Power, Voltas, Indian Hotels (Taj), Air India (re-acquired 2022), Tata Digital (Tata Neu super-app), Tata Electronics (semiconductor/assembly push).
- **Strategic thrust.** "One Tata" simplification (fewer, larger entities), a consumer-facing digital super-app, and a hard pivot into new manufacturing — semiconductors, EV batteries (Agratas), and precision electronics — riding the China+1 and India-manufacturing tailwinds.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** The trust ownership and value system (ethics, long-termism, nation-building) is central to any Tata conversation. Its flagship leadership programme, **TAS (Tata Administrative Service)** — a 12-month rotational fast-track founded 1956 by JRD Tata — is a frequent interview anchor: expect "why Tata / why general management" and questions on cross-company mobility.

### Reliance Industries (RIL)
- **Snapshot.** Founded 1957–66 by Dhirubhai Ambani; chaired by Mukesh Ambani. India's largest and most profitable private company and first to cross ₹10T ($120B+) market cap. Built on textiles → polyester → the Jamnagar refinery (world's largest single-site refining complex).
- **Flagship entities.** O2C (oil-to-chemicals) — the legacy cash engine; **Jio** — the telecom/digital arm that rewired Indian data pricing and is now the connectivity + digital-services platform; **Reliance Retail** — India's largest retailer by revenue and footprint; a fast-scaling **New Energy** business (solar giga-factories, batteries, green hydrogen at Jamnagar).
- **Strategic thrust.** Rotate from a hydrocarbon balance sheet toward consumer (retail + digital) and new energy; monetize Jio and Retail via strategic/PE stakes (Google, Meta, and global PE invested in the 2020 round); a mooted eventual value-unlocking of Jio and Retail.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** Reliance is the canonical "capital-allocation across S-curves" case: a commodity cash cow funding two consumer platforms and an energy transition simultaneously. Expect debate on cross-subsidy, execution at scale, and succession (the three Ambani children now hold defined leadership roles).

### Aditya Birla Group (ABG)
- **Snapshot.** Roots to 1857 (Birla family); global conglomerate HQ'd in Mumbai, ~50% of revenue from overseas, ~187k employees across 40+ countries. Chairman: Kumar Mangalam Birla.
- **Flagship entities.** Hindalco/Novelis (world's largest aluminium-rolling and recycler), UltraTech Cement (India's largest cement-maker), Grasim (VSF, chemicals, and the new Birla Opus paints entry), Aditya Birla Capital (NBFC/insurance/AMC), Aditya Birla Fashion & Retail (Pantaloons, Allen Solly, Peter England, Van Heusen, Louis Philippe), Vodafone Idea (struggling telecom JV).
- **Strategic thrust.** Consolidate leadership in commodities (aluminium, cement), a large greenfield **paints** entry to challenge Asian Paints, and a B2B e-commerce/financial-services digital build. Vodafone Idea is the group's overhang.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** ABG is a metals-and-materials cost-curve reference and a case study in disciplined category leadership. Its leadership programme, **ABGLP** (since 2007), is a common interview route — expect versatility and cross-business-mobility framing.

### Adani Group
- **Snapshot.** Founded 1988 (Gautam Adani) as a commodity-trading firm; Ahmedabad-HQ'd infrastructure conglomerate; flagship Adani Enterprises acts as an in-house incubator that spins mature businesses into separate listcos. Crossed $100B market cap in 2021 (third Indian group to do so).
- **Flagship entities.** Adani Ports & SEZ (India's largest private port operator), Adani Green Energy (one of the world's largest renewables developers), Adani Energy Solutions (transmission), Adani Power (largest private thermal), Adani Enterprises (incubator; airports, roads, data centers, new materials, and the Adani Wilmar edible-oil JV), plus a 7-airport concession portfolio and cement (ACC/Ambuja, acquired 2022).
- **Strategic thrust.** Own India's hard infrastructure spine — ports, power, transmission, airports, roads — and lead the energy transition (a very large green-hydrogen/renewables capex programme), funded by aggressive debt-plus-equity and asset monetization.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** Adani is the modern "infrastructure + capital-intensity + leverage" case. Handle it evenhandedly: enormous execution speed and a genuine infra footprint on one side; leverage, promoter concentration, and the 2023 short-seller episode and governance scrutiny on the other. A strong analysis names both and reasons about debt serviceability and refinancing.

### Mahindra Group (M&M)
- **Snapshot.** Founded 1945 (Mahindra brothers) as a steel-trading firm; ~$20B+ federated conglomerate, ~260k employees, 100+ countries. Chairman: Anand Mahindra.
- **Flagship entities.** Mahindra & Mahindra — India's #1 SUV player by revenue share and the world's largest tractor-maker by volume; Tech Mahindra (IT services); Mahindra Finance (rural NBFC); Mahindra Lifespaces, Mahindra Holidays, Mahindra Logistics; a distinct EV push (BE/XUV electric range).
- **Strategic thrust.** "Federation" operating model (empowered business CEOs under a lean center), leadership in SUVs and farm equipment, and an EV/renewables and premiumization bet. Capital discipline after pruning underperforming international units.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** Mahindra is the reference for **federated org design** and for auto/farm-equipment economics (rural demand cyclicality, tractor seasonality). The "Rise" philosophy and purpose-led brand are frequent interview themes.

### Bajaj Group
- **Snapshot.** Founded 1926, born of the freedom movement (Jamnalal Bajaj). Two power centers today: Bajaj Auto and Bajaj Finserv. Chair figures: Niraj and Sanjiv Bajaj.
- **Flagship entities.** Bajaj Auto (a global two-/three-wheeler leader and export champion — Pulsar, KTM partnership, Chetak EV); Bajaj Finserv holding → Bajaj Finance (India's most valuable NBFC and a consumer-lending juggernaut) and Bajaj Allianz (life + general insurance); Bajaj Electricals.
- **Strategic thrust.** Defend and premiumize two-wheelers while pivoting to EV and exports; scale consumer credit and insurance via Bajaj Finance's data-and-distribution flywheel (EMI cards, POS financing).
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** Bajaj Finance is the canonical Indian **consumer-lending unit-economics** case (customer acquisition cost, cross-sell, NIM, credit costs, GNPA). Bajaj Auto is an exports-and-premiumization story. Governance and family capital allocation are unusually conservative — a contrast worth drawing against more leveraged peers.

### Vedanta / JSW (the metals challengers)
- **Vedanta.** Anil Agarwal's natural-resources group (Vedanta Ltd; London-linked holding history via Vedanta Resources). Zinc (Hindustan Zinc), aluminium, oil & gas (Cairn), iron ore, copper, and a new-age push into semiconductors and display glass. The case here is **commodity cyclicality plus holdco leverage and a proposed demerger** into pure-play verticals — a structural-simplification and debt-management story.
- **JSW Group.** Sajjan Jindal's group; JSW Steel (among India's largest steelmakers), JSW Energy, JSW Infrastructure, JSW Cement, and a new EV/auto ambition (MG Motor JV). The thrust is aggressive capacity build across steel/energy/infra and vertical integration. Together with Tata Steel, SAIL, AM/NS, and Vedanta, these set the **domestic steel cost curve** — your benchmark set for any metals engagement.

### Piramal Group
- **Snapshot.** Ajay Piramal's Mumbai group; philosophy "Doing Well and Doing Good." Reinvented itself twice — famously selling its domestic formulations business to Abbott (2010) at a landmark valuation, then redeploying capital.
- **Flagship entities.** Piramal Pharma (CDMO, complex hospital generics, consumer health), Piramal Enterprises / Piramal Finance (retail + wholesale lending, post the DHFL acquisition), and alternative-investments/real-estate-credit interests.
- **Strategic thrust.** Build a diversified retail-lending franchise while running a global pharma-services business; disciplined buy-fix-monetize capital cycling.
- **What it tests / how to talk about it.** Piramal is a **capital-allocation and portfolio-reshaping** case — when to exit at the top (Abbott deal) and how to redeploy. Useful analogue for "what would you do with the proceeds" questions.

### RPG Group / Bharti (Airtel) — and the rest
- **RPG Enterprises.** Harsh Goenka's ~$4.4B group of 15+ companies: CEAT (tyres), KEC International (EPC/transmission), Zensar (IT), RPG Life Sciences. A mid-cap, professionally managed federation; good contrast case to the mega-houses.
- **Bharti Airtel.** Sunil Bharti Mittal's telecom champion (founded 1995), HQ New Delhi; a leading global telco across India and Africa, plus Airtel Payments Bank and a digital-services layer. The reference for **telecom economics** in India — ARPU repair, spectrum capex, the 3-player market structure (Jio, Airtel, Vodafone Idea), and the 5G monetization question.
- **Others worth holding.** CK Birla Group (HIL, Orient, Birlasoft; diversified mid-cap), and TAS-adjacent leadership pipelines. When a case names a group you don't recognize, apply the same skeleton: *ownership → flagship cash engine → capital-allocation logic → governance watch-out.*

## Cross-cutting patterns (reason with these)

1. **Promoter control + professional management.** Most houses are family-controlled but increasingly run by professional CEOs under a family chair. Ask: *where does real decision authority sit — family, holdco board, or operating CEO?* This drives stakeholder mapping on any engagement (see `references/client-political-mapping.md`).
2. **Holdco / cross-holding structures.** Value and control often sit in an unlisted parent (Tata Sons, Bajaj Holdings, Adani Enterprises as incubator). Cross-subsidy and related-party dynamics matter for both diligence and strategy. Watch **promoter share-pledging** as a leverage-and-risk signal.
3. **The cash-cow-funds-the-transition pattern.** Reliance (O2C → retail/digital/energy), Adani (ports/power → green hydrogen), Tata (TCS → new manufacturing). Nearly every house is redeploying a mature cash engine into a capital-intensive new S-curve. The strategic question is almost always *capital allocation and pacing.*
4. **Leadership-development programmes as talent moats.** TAS (Tata), ABGLP (Aditya Birla), Mahindra's and RPG's GM tracks — rotational fast-tracks that seed the next generation of group leaders. Relevant both as an org-capability lever and as the frame for general-management interviews.
5. **Governance is the diligence swing factor.** Concentration, leverage, related-party transactions, rating-agency reliance, and disclosure quality separate the houses. The five governance-failure case studies in `case-studies/` (Satyam, IL&FS, Kingfisher, Harshad Mehta, insider-trading) are the pattern library — read `references/due-diligence-deep-dive.md` → *Governance & Fraud Red Flags* alongside this file.

## Using this in an interview or engagement

- **General-management / leadership-programme interview.** Lead with the house's *value system and strategic thrust*, then tie your motivation to a specific business and a specific programme (TAS/ABGLP/etc.). Have one crisp "recent development" per target house (a major acquisition, a capex announcement, a new-business entry).
- **Strategy / market-entry case.** Identify the incumbent house from the house map first; assume it will cross-subsidize and defend. Frame your entrant's wedge against the house's structural advantages (distribution, capital, brand umbrella).
- **Diligence / credit case.** Go straight to structure and leverage: holdco vs. opco, promoter pledging, related-party flows, and refinancing runway. Name both the execution strength and the governance watch-out — a one-sided read is a junior read.
